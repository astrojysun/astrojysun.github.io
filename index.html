About Me

I am a PhD candidate in Astronomy at the Ohio State University (advisor: Adam Leroy). I am also an active member of the PHANGS collaboration, and a large part of my research is within the scope of this collaboration. Before joining OSU, I received a B.Sc. in Astrophysics at Tsinghua University in 2015.

My research interest is on characterizing and understanding the fundamental properties of the molecular interstellar medium (ISM) in nearby galaxies. My dissertation work features a uniform analysis of high resolution, high sensitivity, millimeter-wave observations covering a large sample of galaxies. The majority of these observations were acquired as part of the PHANGS-ALMA survey (PI: E. Schinnerer). Pairing them with new and archival multi-wavelength data, I put the molecular ISM into the context of its ambient galactic environment, and unveil how its properties are related to star formation, stellar feedback, and dynamical processes.

In my leisure time, I love listening to classic music and playing piano. Beethoven, Schubert, Brahms, and Mahler are among my favorite composers. I also enjoy reading books on philosophy of science and psychology, or random novels at times.

My Research

Mapping Molecular Clouds across the Local Star-forming Galaxy Population

I am leading the efforts on measuring fundamental properties of molecular clouds in an unprecedented sample of nearby galaxies, using the PHANGS-ALMA CO(2-1) imaging data. This new dataset provides so far the best synthetic view of molecular cloud properties across the local star-forming galaxy population. My paper in 2018 presented a systematic analysis of ~30,000 molecular cloud measurements in 15 galaxies. My second paper in 2020 extended this analysis to ~100,000 molecular cloud measurements across 70 galaxies.

Putting Molecular Clouds in the Context of Their Natal Environment

Based on a joint analysis of the PHANGS-ALMA CO dataset and a multiwavelength supporting dataset, I found strong evidence that molecular clouds were connected to their natal environment in the host galaxy. My first paper in 2020 showed that the molecular cloud populations in star-forming galaxy disks peferred a dynamical equilibrium state set by the interplay between gas pressures and gravitational forces. This study highlighted several key mechanisms that shaped the properties of molecular clouds and regulated star formation in a wide range of galactic environments.

Measuring Star Formation Efficiency in the Molecular ISM in Galaxies

I made key contributions to a synthetic work led by my colleague Dyas Utomo in 2018. We found that star formation was inefficient in the molecular ISM in 14 nearby galaxies. Across this sample, we estimated a median value of 0.7% for the star formation efficiency per free-fall time --- a key dimensionless parameter incorporated in many star formation models.

Connecting Observations with Numerical Simulations

I helped with carrying out a direct comparison between the CO observations of M33 and a "bespoke" numerical simulation of the same galaxy. We saw good agreement between the properties of molecular clouds seen in the simulation and in the real M33. We identified the gas and stellar mass distribution as a main determinant of cloud properties in a galaxy.
