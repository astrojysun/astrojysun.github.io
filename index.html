About Me

I am a PhD candidate (advisor: Adam Leroy) and a graduate presidential fellow at Ohio State University. I am also an active member of the PHANGS collaboration, and a large part of my research is within the scope of this collaboration. Before joining OSU, I received a B.Sc. in Astrophysics at Tsinghua University in 2015.

My dissertation project is on characterizing and understanding the fundamental properties of the molecular interstellar medium (ISM) in nearby galaxies. This work features a uniform analysis of high resolution, high sensitivity, millimeter-wave observations covering a large sample of galaxies. The majority of these observations were acquired as part of the PHANGS-ALMA survey (PI: E. Schinnerer). Pairing them with new and archival multi-wavelength data, I put the molecular ISM into the context of its ambient galactic environment, and unveil how its properties are related to star formation, stellar feedback, and dynamical processes.

Selected Research Projects

Measuring Molecular Gas Properties on Cloud Scales in Nearby Galaxies

I am leading the efforts on measuring the fundamental properties of molecular gas (surface density, velocity dispersion, dynamical state, and internal pressure) using the PHANGS-ALMA CO(2-1) imaging data. The high resolution and high sensitivity of these data grant us access to the distribution and kinematics of molecular gas on 50-100 parsec scales (i.e., "cloud scales"). In 2018, I presented a systematic analysis of the CO data for 15 galaxies, which include the PHANGS-ALMA pilot sample (ADS, Youtube). Now I am extending this analysis to the entire PHANGS-ALMA sample of ~80 galaxies.

Molecular Clouds in the Context of Their (Galactic) Neighbourhood

Based on a joint analysis of the PHANGS-ALMA CO data and a set of rich, multi-wavelength supporting data, I find strong evidence that molecular clouds are closely connected to their immediate surroundings in the host galaxy. In a recently submitted paper, I specifically tested a common hypothesis that molecular clouds exist in dynamical equilibrium inside the local gravitational potential. This study highlighted several key mechanisms shaping the properties of the molecular cloud population and regulating star formation activity in a wide range of galactic environments.

Star Formation Efficiency in the Molecular ISM in Nearby Galaxies

I made key contributions to a synthetic work (ADS) led by my colleague Dyas Utomo in 2018. There we found that star formation was inefficient in the molecular ISM in 14 nearby galaxies. We estimated a median of 0.7% for the star formation efficiency per free-fall time --- a key dimensionless parameter incorporated in many star formation models.

Connecting Observations with Numerical Simulations

I helped with carrying out a direct comparison (ADS) between the CO observations of M33 and a "bespoke" numerical simulation of the same galaxy. We saw good agreement between the properties of molecular clouds seen in the simulation and in the real M33. We identified the gas and stellar mass distribution in a galaxy as the main determinant of cloud properties.
